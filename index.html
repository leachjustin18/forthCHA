They are from a small town two hours away, and you are unsure of where they are staying for the night. As evening comes and it is time for change of shift, you note they seem anxious. You would like to help them feel more comfortable. Proper interventions to help in their time of crisis may include: select all that apply.
Arrange for language, cultural or religious support during times of high stress. Inquire about family needs, such as lodging, transportation, child care, etc. Work with the social worker to assist with these needs. Assist parents with formulation of questions and concerns.

These are all proper interventions to assist the family in crisis. Other interventions include:

  • Provide opportunities for the parents to vent feelings and concerns, allowing them to express negative feelings without being judged.
  • Recognize and identify the family's strengths.
  • Help family's transitions to life in the ICU.

Tatum delivered her infant daughter, Piper, at 28 weeks. Piper was immediately admitted to the NICU. Tatum is emotional and worried not only about her own recovery, but the care of her daughter. Appropriate interventions to assist her and other parents include:
  • Take the parents on a tour of the unit, or link to other resources (video or web site) when not possible.
  • Provide booklets to the family which describes the NICU and the professionals involved in their infant's care.
  • Create a welcoming and warm unit environment.
  • Do not overwhelm the parents with details during the initial visit to the ICU. The parents will appreciate and require detailed information at a later time.
  • Be attentive to the mother's physical needs post–delivery.
Staffing changes, monitors and equipment, and the deluge of medical information can be overwhelming to parents in the ICU. In order to decrease anxiety and frustration, it is helpful to:
  • Provide families with the names of the staff members providing care to their child.
  • Help families transition with the rotation of doctors and nurses.
  • Provide consistent caregivers whenever possible.
  • Be consistent with the information given to parents.
  • Arrange for family meetings between family and staff members (e.g., physicians, nurses, social worker and chaplain).
  • Provide continuous updates and information related to the infant's condition via phone or directly. Provide the parents a toll–free number to make long–distance calls to the ICU, if available. If the family does not have a phone due to financial issues, consult with social services.
